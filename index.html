Heroin

What is Heroin?

Heroin is an addictive drug made from morphine, a psychoactive (mind-altering) substance taken from the resin of the seed pod of the opium poppy plant. The color and look of this drug depends on how it is made and what else it is mixed with. It can be white or brown powder or a black, sticky substance called black tar heroin. It is part of the class of drugs called opioids and is mixed with water, injected with a needle, sniffed, smoked or snorted.
People who take heroin sometimes combine it with other drugs, such as alcohol or cocaine which can be very dangerous and raise the risk of overdose.
When mixed with alcohol, the user can get in a coma or have dangerously slowed (or even stopped) breathing that can eventually lead to death.
Smack, Junk, Black tar, Ska and Horse are some of the common street names for Heroin.

The Impact of Heroin on the Brain and Body

When heroin enters the brain, it attaches to the molecules on the cells known as opioid receptors. These receptors are located in many areas of the brain and body, especially areas involved in the perception of pain and pleasure, as well as a part of the brain that regulates breathing.
Some effects of heroin include a rush of good feelings and clouded thinking and can last for a few hours. When that happens, people feel drowsy, their heart rate and breathing slow down but when the effects start wearing off, the user experiences a depressed mood and is tempted to use the drug to regain the good feelings.
Another factor associated with taking Heroin repeatedly is:
Addiction: People can have a tough time stopping the use of Heroin if they really want to and even after it causes horrific consequences to their health. Since it can cause changes to the brain’s functions after using it repeatedly, the user will feel like something bad will happen to them if they don’t take the drug again. This trait is what makes the drug the most addictive of all.
Heroin bought on the street often contains a mix of substances, including a dangerous opioid called fentanyl. Drug dealers add fentanyl because it is cheap which helps them to save money. Some of these substances can be toxic and can even clog the blood vessels of the lungs, liver, kidney or brain which can cause permanent damage to these organs.
Also, sharing drug injection equipment or engaging in risky behaviors can increase the risk of being exposed to diseases such as HIV and hepatitis(inflammation of the liver).

Short Term Effects

Opioid receptors are located in the brain, the brain stem, down the spinal cord, in the lungs and intestines. Due to the location of the receptors, using heroin can result in a wide variety of physical problems related to breathing and other basic life functions.
Some of the ways Heroin affects the user's body is:

  • Dry mouth

  • Arms and legs feel heavy

  • Stomach feels sick and throwing up

  • A severe case of itching

  • Mind is clouded, not thinking clearly.

  • Brief period of intense happiness

  • Body goes in and out of consciousness repetitively.

  • An increased risk of HIV that goes through shared needles with heroin.

Long Term Effects

  • Sleeping problems

  • Damaged tissues in the nose if user sniffs/snorts heroin

  • Stomach/constipation cramping

  • Liver and kidney disease

  • Lung problems

  • Behavioral changes - The user may gain mental health problems like depression.

Fun Facts

  • There is no cookie cutter heroin user- In fact, many of heroin’s newest addicts are in their teens, early 20s or come from middle- or upper-middle-class suburban families.

  • When an addict stops using, he or she experiences physical withdrawal which can begin within just a few hours since the last use.

  • Major withdrawal symptoms peak between 48 and 72 hours after the last dose and can last up to a week. For some withdrawal can be as long as a few months after stopping the drug.Sudden withdrawal by heavily dependent users can be fatal.

  • Heroin was first manufactured in 1898 by the Bayer pharmaceutical company of Germany and marketed as a treatment for tuberculosis as well as a remedy for morphine addiction.

  • Approximately 13.5 million people in the world take opioids, including 9.2 million who use heroin.
